## Paper Primer

### One‑Trillion‑Parameter Scientific Generalist

**Intern‑S1‑Pro** hits the trillion‑parameter mark and outperforms closed‑source giants on scientific reasoning – 55.5 vs 14.7 on *SciReasoner*.

### Why You Need a Scientific Multimodal Model

If you’ve ever tried to coax a language model into interpreting a microscopy image or predicting a material’s band gap, you know the current tools either hallucinate or choke on domain‑specific notation. You need a single system that can read papers, understand plots, and even plan experiments without swapping models every time.

### What’s Broken in Today’s Landscape

- **Domain‑specific models stay narrow** – Galactica, ChemBERTa, etc. excel on a single task but collapse when you ask a different scientific question. 
- **Multimodal VLMs ignore physical signal structure** – RoPE or sinusoidal encodings flatten images and spectra, losing wave‑like information. 
- **Mixture‑of‑Experts (MoE) at scale is unstable** – traditional Top‑K routing creates severe load imbalance, causing OOM crashes and poor convergence. 
- **Reinforcement‑learning fine‑tuning blows up memory** – full‑precision FP16/BF16 on a trillion‑parameter MoE is infeasible. 
- **Scientific caption data are scarce** – public web alt‑texts are short, noisy, and rarely aligned with dense scientific figures.

### The Core Idea in Plain English

We took **Intern‑S1**, expanded its expert pool four‑fold, and gave every GPU a *balanced* slice of the MoE via **Grouped Routing**. Then we taught the model to see the world as a *continuous wave* instead of a string of discrete tokens by replacing RoPE with **Fourier Position Encoding (FoPE)**. On top of that we built a **time‑series encoder** that adaptively chops long signals into manageable patches, and we fed the model **≈270 B tokens of high‑quality scientific image‑text pairs** generated by a PDF‑to‑caption pipeline. Finally, we fine‑tuned everything with a **mixed‑precision RL loop** that quantises only the massive expert linear layers to FP8, keeps the language head in FP32, and uses a dual‑importance‑sampling loss to stay on‑policy. The result is a single trillion‑parameter model that can reason about chemistry, read microscopy images, and plan a multi‑step experiment—all without swapping out components. **That’s it. That’s the core idea.**

### Why a Trillion‑Parameter Scientific Model?

Scientific research spans dozens of highly specialized subfields—chemistry, biology, physics, earth sciences—each with its own symbols, datasets, and reasoning patterns. Just as multilingual translation systems had to grow roughly **90×** larger to handle hundreds of language pairs, a foundation model that aims to master the full breadth of scientific knowledge must expand its capacity dramatically. A **one‑trillion‑parameter** model therefore provides the representational power needed to jointly tackle **over 100 distinct scientific tasks** while still retaining general language and vision abilities.

### Intern‑S1‑Pro and the **SAGE** Framework

**Intern‑S1‑Pro** is the first trillion‑parameter scientific multimodal foundation model, offering stronger reasoning, image‑text understanding, and autonomous agent capabilities for complex scientific workflows. It is built on the **SAGE** (Synergistic Architecture for Generalizable Experts) framework, which organizes the system into three layers:

- **Foundation** – core knowledge base shared across domains 
- **Fusion** – cross‑domain integration of language and vision 
- **Evolution** – task‑specific specialization for expert performance

### Core Challenges and Engineering Solutions

Scaling to a trillion parameters introduces two major architectural hurdles:

1. **Training instability from extreme expert load imbalance** – with millions of experts, some receive far more tokens than others, causing memory spikes. 
2. **Difficulty optimizing router embeddings** – a vastly expanded expert pool makes conventional router updates inefficient.

To overcome these, the authors propose:

- **Group routing** – enforces a lower bound on the number of tokens assigned to each expert, guaranteeing balanced loads and stabilizing training. 
- **Gradient estimation scheme** – accelerates router‑embedding updates by providing more frequent, low‑variance gradient signals.

On the systems side, the **XTuner** training framework and **LMDeploy** inference engine are co‑designed to preserve high throughput, resulting in only **~20 %** efficiency loss despite a **4×** increase in model size. This tight algorithm‑infrastructure integration demonstrates that trillion‑scale scientific models can be trained and deployed practically.

### MoE Intuition and Expert Expansion 

The **Mixture‑of‑Experts (MoE)** layer lets a transformer keep inference cheap while scaling its capacity by housing many expert feed‑forward networks. For each token, a lightweight router decides which few experts should process it, so only a sparse subset is active.

* **Token routing** – A token represented by a vector **x is a d-dimensional vector of real numbers** is fed to a router that computes scores **z = W_router · x**, where **W_router is an N×d matrix of real numbers** and **N** is the total number of experts. The router then picks the top‑ **K** scores, forming the sparse set **the set S** of experts that will be evaluated. 

* **Why sparsity matters** – Using all **N** experts for every token would make the model as expensive as a dense network. The Top‑ **K** selection preserves efficiency while still allowing the model to draw on a huge pool of specialized experts. 

* **Concrete example** – The token “cat” might be routed to an expert that knows animal semantics and another that handles visual grounding, while the remaining experts stay idle. 

* **Expert expansion** – Intern‑S1‑Pro starts from a trained Intern‑S1 model and **duplicates each expert**. This simple duplication multiplies the expert pool without altering the router logic. The duplicated experts are later organized into groups (see the next subsection) to keep training stable.

### Grouped Routing for Load Balancing 

**Key takeaway:** By partitioning experts into device‑aligned groups and selecting one expert per group, Intern‑S1‑Pro guarantees that every device processes the same amount of work, eliminating the classic MoE load‑imbalance problem.

* **Definitions** – Let **E** be the total number of experts and **S** the degree of expert parallelism (i.e., the number of devices). Experts are split uniformly into **G** disjoint groups **the set of expert groups {E₁,..., E_G}**, each containing **E/G** experts and mapped to a single device. 

* **Intra‑group selection** – Within each group **g**, the router picks the highest‑scoring **K/G** experts. The final activated set is the union 
 
 \bigcup_{g=1}^{G}\text{Top}_{K/G}(\mathcal{E}_{g}).
 
 *Where:* 
 - **the union of all sets** – union over all groups 
 - **the Top K/G experts in group g** – the **K/G** experts with highest router scores inside group **g** 

 This step ensures that each group contributes exactly **K/G** experts to the forward pass. 

* **Intern‑S1‑Pro 1T configuration** – The model uses **k=8** and an EP8 strategy ( **S=8**). Consequently **G=8** and **K/G = 1**, so **each device activates exactly one expert** (Top‑1 within its group). 

* **Why Top‑1 per group yields absolute load balancing** – Because each group is tied to a single device, picking exactly one expert per group forces every device to evaluate the same number of experts, regardless of how the token distribution varies across the batch.

*Figure 3 shows that with **k=8** under EP8, the Grouped Router spreads tokens evenly across the eight devices, removing the out‑of‑memory spikes seen with traditional Top‑K routing.*

### Straight‑Through Estimator for Sparse Routing 

**Key takeaway:** The Straight‑Through Estimator (STE) decouples the sparse forward pass from a dense backward pass, ensuring that *all* experts receive gradient information even when they are not selected.

1. **Sparse forward computation** 
 
 \mathbf{y} = \sum_{i \in \mathcal{S}} \tilde{p}_i \cdot E_i(\mathbf{x}),
 
 *Where:* 
 - **output vector y** – output of the MoE layer for the current token 
 - **the set of selected experts S** – set of experts chosen by Top‑ **K** routing 
 - **normalized probability weight for expert i** – normalized softmax probability of expert **i** within **the set of selected experts S** 
 - **output of expert i for input x** – feed‑forward output of expert **i** applied to input **input vector x** 

 Only the experts in **the set of selected experts S** contribute to the forward pass, keeping compute low.

2. **STE construction** – To give every expert a learning signal, the routing weight used in back‑propagation is 
 
 \hat{p}_i^{\text{STE}} = \underbrace{\text{sg}(m_i \cdot p_i^\tau)}_{\text{forward (sparse)}} + \underbrace{(p_i^\tau - \text{sg}(p_i^\tau))}_{\text{backward (dense)}},
 
 *Where:* 
 - **Straight-Through Estimator probability for expert i** – STE routing weight for expert **i** 
 - **stop-gradient operator** – stop‑gradient operator (blocks gradients) 
 - **binary mask m_i (either 0 or 1)** – mask indicating whether **expert index i is in the selected set S** (1) or not (0) 
 - **softened probability for expert i with temperature tau** – temperature‑scaled softmax probability, **p_i^tau = softmax(z/tau) for index i** 
 - The first term is used in the forward pass (keeps the sparse mask), while the second term supplies a dense gradient during back‑propagation. 

 *Why it works* – The forward path behaves exactly like ordinary Top‑ **K** routing, but the backward path sees the full softmax distribution, so gradients flow to every expert.

3. **Gradient propagation** – Differentiating the loss **loss function L** w.r.t. a router logit **logit value z_j** yields 
 
 \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial z_{j}}=\sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \hat{p}_{i}^{\mathrm{STE}}} \cdot \frac{\partial p_{i}^{\tau}}{\partial z_{j}}.
 
 *Where:* 
 - **partial derivative of loss L with respect to logit z_j** – gradient of the loss with respect to logit **logit value z_j** 
 - **N** – total number of experts 
 - **partial derivative of loss L with respect to the STE probability for expert i** – upstream gradient from the loss to the STE weight of expert **i** 
 - **partial derivative of softened probability p_i^tau with respect to logit z_j** – element of the softmax Jacobian, non‑zero for all **(i,j)** 

 Because the softmax Jacobian is dense, **every expert receives a non‑zero gradient**, even if it was masked out in the forward pass.

*Example* – Suppose a token selects expert 3 (so **mask for expert 3 equals 1 (selected)**). Expert 7 has **mask for expert 7 equals 0 (not selected)**, yet the term **partial derivative of softened probability for expert 7 with respect to logit z_j** appears in the sum, allowing expert 7 to be updated.

![](https://storage.googleapis.com/pg_image_bucket/papers/20260329-133331-2603-25040-s-0e289e/figure2.png) 
*Figure 2 (right side) visualises this split: the forward path keeps the sparse Top‑K mask, while the backward path flows through the full softmax distribution.*

### Vision Encoder Overview 

Intern‑S1‑Pro employs a native **Vision Transformer (ViT)** as its visual front‑end. The ViT processes images at their original resolution, producing a number of visual tokens proportional to the input size rather than a fixed grid. These tokens retain fine‑grained spatial detail and are passed through a multilayer perceptron (MLP) projector that aligns the visual feature dimension with the language model’s embedding space. 

The vision encoder is contrastively pre‑trained on a massive multimodal corpus—English datasets (CC12M, LAION‑COCO, SBU Caption) and Chinese datasets (LAION‑2B‑Multi, Wukong)—totaling roughly **300 million image–text pairs**. This pre‑training yields strong visual representations and tight cross‑modal alignment, enabling effective multimodal reasoning downstream.

### Fourier Position Encoding (FoPE) 

**Key takeaway:** FoPE replaces a single sinusoidal positional signal with a learnable Fourier series per dimension, capturing both low‑ and high‑frequency positional patterns that better reflect the wave‑like nature of physical signals.

Traditional encodings (sinusoidal, RoPE) inject only a single frequency, ignoring the rich spectral content of real‑world data such as light or sound. FoPE models each embedding dimension **d** as a sum of multiple sinusoidal components:


\sum_{f=1}^{F} a_{d,f}\sin(2\pi f\,\text{pos}) + b_{d,f}\cos(2\pi f\,\text{pos}),
 

*Where:* 
- **F** – number of frequency bands used for the Fourier series 
- **a_d,f and b_d,f** – learnable amplitudes for the sine and cosine terms of frequency **f** in dimension **d** 
- **pos** – token position index 
- **sin** and **cos** – standard trigonometric functions 

A single sinusoid captures only one frequency, missing the nuanced positional cues present in real signals. By combining many frequencies, the model can represent **fine‑grained (high‑frequency) and coarse (low‑frequency) positional patterns** simultaneously.

![](https://storage.googleapis.com/pg_image_bucket/papers/20260329-133331-2603-25040-s-0e289e/figure4.png) 
*Figure 4 shows each dimension decomposed into multiple frequency components; poorly trained frequencies are clipped to prevent harmful spectral interference.*

### Time‑Series Encoder and Adaptive Subsampling 

**Key takeaway:** Adaptive subsampling automatically adjusts patch granularity based on the signal’s sampling rate, preserving high‑frequency details while keeping the number of tokens tractable.

* **Challenges** – Time‑series data vary wildly in length and sampling rate. A fixed tokenization either yields an unmanageably long sequence or discards important dynamics. 

* **Two‑stage pipeline** – 
 1. **Adaptive subsampling block** – The raw continuous signal is split into local patches. Patch length **L** and stride **S** are computed on‑the‑fly from the signal’s sampling frequency so that the resulting number of patches **N_patch** stays within a target window (e.g., **10²** – **10⁶**). 
 2. **Time‑series encoder** – Each patch is encoded locally (e.g., with a small convolution or MLP) to capture short‑term dynamics. The sequence of patch embeddings is then fed to a transformer‑style encoder that models long‑range dependencies across patches. 

* **Why adaptive subsampling works** – For high‑frequency signals the module chooses a smaller **L** and stride, satisfying the Nyquist criterion and retaining rapid variations. For low‑frequency signals it uses larger patches, reducing token count without losing essential structure.

### Scale of Pre‑training and Why Scientific Captioning Matters 

Intern‑S1‑Pro continues pre‑training on a massive **6 trillion** token budget of image‑text and pure‑text data. The crucial upgrade over its predecessor is the injection of a large corpus of scientific captions. Scientific images differ sharply from everyday photographs: they contain fine‑grained diagrams, plots, and formulae that demand precise, domain‑specific interpretation. Public repositories host countless high‑quality scientific figures, yet the accompanying captions are typically terse, loosely aligned, and written as ancillary notes rather than full descriptions. The caption pipeline is therefore built to turn these raw pairs into dense, well‑aligned image‑text examples that strengthen cross‑modal supervision for scientific visual content.

### PDF‑Driven Caption Pipeline Architecture 

The end‑to‑end workflow that creates the scientific caption dataset is illustrated in **Figure 7**. 

![](https://storage.googleapis.com/pg_image_bucket/papers/20260329-133331-2603-25040-s-0e289e/figure7.png) 
*Figure 7 visualizes the pipeline, showing how high‑quality aligned scientific multimodal data is produced efficiently.*

The process unfolds in six stages:

1. **PDF ingestion** – Massive collections of scientific PDFs are streamed as the primary source of visual material. 
2. **Layout analysis** – MinerU2.5 scans each page, detecting figures, formulas, and tables, and records their bounding boxes. 
3. **Standardized cropping** – Detected regions are cut out into uniform sub‑images, providing a consistent input size for downstream models. 
4. **Deduplication** – Perceptual hashing (pHash) identifies visually identical crops; duplicates are discarded so each unique visual element appears only once. 
5. **Topic classification** – A lightweight classifier examines visual cues (axes, chemical structures, equation symbols) and decides whether a sub‑image is scientific or general. 
6. **Caption generation** – 
 * **Scientific sub‑images** → routed to **InternVL3.5‑241B**, a vision‑language model fine‑tuned for professional, domain‑specific descriptions. 
 * **Non‑scientific sub‑images** → routed to **CapRL‑32B**, built on the Qwen 2.5 VL 32B backbone and trained with Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) to encourage dense, high‑quality captions.

### Dual‑Model Routing: Scientific vs. General Images 

**Key takeaway:** Using two specialized caption generators prevents a one‑size‑fits‑all model from compromising either scientific precision or linguistic diversity. 

The topic‑classification module evaluates each cropped region for visual markers of scientific content—axes, chemical structures, or LaTeX‑style symbols. If such markers are present, the image is sent to InternVL3.5‑241B; otherwise, CapRL‑32B handles it.

### Ensuring Linguistic Diversity and Quality 

To avoid over‑fitting to a narrow scientific style, the pipeline employs a **multi‑template randomized prompting** strategy. For each caption request, a template is randomly selected from a pool of sentence patterns, injecting syntactic variety while preserving factual content. 

Generated captions then pass through a **0.5 billion** ‑parameter text quality discriminator** that filters out:

- Garbled or nonsensical text 
- Repetitive phrasing 
- Low‑information‑density sentences 

After these safeguards, the curated scientific caption corpus totals roughly ****270 billion** tokens. This massive, dense supervisory signal dramatically enriches the training of large‑scale vision‑language models, especially for tasks requiring fine‑grained visual reasoning.

### Transforming Structured Scientific Data into Narrative 

Scientific information often arrives in rigid formats—tables, chemical formulas, or database entries—that cannot be directly mixed with free‑form prose. The pipeline therefore performs two complementary transformations:

1. **Template Construction** – Structured rows are mapped to natural sentences. 
 *Example:* A table row “H₂O, 18 g/mol, 100 °C” becomes “Water has a molecular weight of 18 g/mol and boils at 100 °C.” 

2. **Task Form Transformation** – Outputs lacking intuitive semantics (e.g., a matrix of reaction yields) are converted using domain‑specific priors. 
 *Example:* A matrix entry “85 %” for reaction A is rendered as “The yield of reaction A is 85 %.” 

These steps preserve the original semantics, prevent information loss, and align scientific data with the narrative style of general text, enabling seamless joint training.

--- 

### System Prompt Isolation to Prevent Negative Transfer 

**Negative transfer** occurs when mixing scientific and general data causes a **distribution shift**: the model’s parameters become confused between the deterministic logic of scientific inputs and the richer, more ambiguous semantics of everyday language. 

**System Prompt Isolation** solves this by prepending mutually exclusive system‑level prefixes:

- Scientific samples receive a prefix such as **`[SCIENTIFIC_MODE]`**. 
- General samples receive a different prefix, e.g., **`[GENERAL_MODE]`**. 

These prefixes create independent contextual processing environments during training, ensuring that the model does not conflate the two domains. Empirically, this isolation yields:

- Reduced training instability and fewer conflict‑induced errors. 
- Improved performance on both scientific and general multimodal benchmarks. 
- More reliable inference, with the model maintaining logical consistency on deterministic scientific tasks while retaining expressive flexibility on general tasks.

### Memory and Precision Challenges in Trillion‑Parameter RL 

The RL phase for **Intern‑S1‑Pro** expands the expert count fourfold, pushing the model into the **trillion‑parameter** regime. Even though each token still activates only a tiny fraction of experts, the sheer number of expert‑layer parameters and their activations overwhelms GPU memory under expert parallelism. 

To keep training feasible the system adopts **FP8 quantization** for the RL phase. FP8 stores each value in **8 bits**, cutting memory usage by more than half and increasing arithmetic throughput because modern tensor cores can process FP8 at twice the speed of BF16. 

However, the model’s extreme sparsity makes naïve FP8 risky: low‑precision rounding can corrupt the few critical values that drive expert selection and normalization, leading to divergence. Consequently, a **targeted mixed‑precision strategy** is required, where only the most memory‑intensive kernels run in FP8 while numerically fragile components stay in higher precision.

---

### Identifying Numerically Sensitive Operators 

A systematic operator‑by‑operator comparison between the **LMDeploy rollout engine** and the **XTuner training engine** revealed three kernels that are especially vulnerable to precision gaps:

- **RMSNorm** – scales activations by the inverse square root of a variance term; FP8 rounding can distort this scaling and cause gradient explosion or vanishing. 
- **Router softmax** – computes exponentials of logits; FP8’s limited dynamic range can overflow or underflow, producing incorrect expert‑selection probabilities. 
- **Positional‑embedding addition** – adds small positional offsets to large token embeddings; rounding errors in FP8 can shift token representations enough to affect downstream attention. 

Reducing the precision mismatch in these kernels is the first step toward stabilizing mixed‑precision RL.

---

### Targeted Mixed‑Precision Scheme for Sparse MoE 

**Key insight:** Expert MLP layers dominate memory consumption, yet their dense matrix‑multiply (GEMM) kernels tolerate reduced precision without harming convergence. 

The precision allocation is therefore:

- **Expert linear layers** → **FP8** (memory‑heavy GEMMs). 
- **Non‑expert components** (RMSNorm, router softmax, embeddings) → **BF16** (balanced precision and speed). 
- **Language‑modeling head** → **FP32** (log‑probability calculations). 

**Why FP32 for the LM head?** The head produces log‑probabilities that feed directly into the policy‑gradient update; even tiny errors are amplified by the REINFORCE estimator, potentially destabilizing learning. 

By confining FP8 to the memory‑intensive expert layers and preserving higher precision where numerical fidelity matters, the scheme retains most of FP8’s memory and throughput benefits while protecting the fragile parts of the computation graph.

---

### Ensuring Expert Routing Consistency via Rollout Router Replay 

**Problem:** Distribution shift arises when the routing decisions during rollout differ from those during training, causing mismatched expert activations. 

**Solution:** *Rollout router replay* records the exact expert indices selected for each token in every layer during the rollout phase. During the subsequent policy‑gradient update, the training engine **replays** these recorded indices verbatim, guaranteeing that the same experts are activated. 

**Engineering trick:** To avoid turning the routing trace into a bandwidth bottleneck, the traces are transmitted using **Ray object references** rather than the HTTP response channel that carries generated tokens. This sidesteps latency and keeps the replay overhead negligible.

---

### Dual Importance‑Sampling for Distribution‑Shift Mitigation 

The REINFORCE objective is augmented with two importance‑sampling ratios and a masking function to curb variance caused by rollout‑training mismatches.

The loss is 

$\mathcal{L}(\theta) = -\mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}, \{y_i\}_{i=1}^{G} \sim \pi_{\theta}^{\text{rollout}}(\cdot|x)} \left[ \frac{1}{G} \sum_{i=1}^{G} \frac{1}{|y_i|} \sum_{t=1}^{|y_i|} \operatorname{sg}\bigl(\mathcal{M}(\rho_{i,t}; \alpha, \beta) \, r_{i,t}\bigr) \, \hat{A}_{i,t} \, \log \pi_{\theta}(y_{i,t} \mid x, y_{i,<t}) \right]$

**Where:** 

- **Loss function with respect to θ** – the scalar loss we minimize. 
- **x sampled from distribution D** – a prompt sampled from the data distribution. 
- **a set of G rollouts y₁ to y_G** – **G** responses generated by the **rollout policy** **the rollout policy parameterized by θ**. 
- **1 divided by G** – averages over the **G** sampled responses. 
- **1 divided by the length of rollout y_i** – averages over tokens in response **the i-th rollout sequence**. 
- **stop-gradient operator** – the **stop‑gradient** operator, preventing gradients from flowing through the importance weights. 
- **mask function of rho_i,t with parameters α and β** – the **masking function** (defined below). 
- **reward or ratio term at rollout i, time step t** – the **new‑policy probability** **probability of token y_i,t under the new policy given context x and previous tokens**. 
- **advantage estimate for rollout i at time step t** – the **advantage estimator** for token **t** in response **i**. 
- **log probability of token y_i,t under policy θ** – log‑probability under the current policy, the quantity REINFORCE differentiates.

In words, the loss scales each token’s log‑probability gradient by a product of (1) a mask that discards outliers, (2) the probability of the token under the current policy, and (3) an advantage that measures how much better the sampled response is compared to its peers.

The **masking function** is 



**mask function equals rho_i,t if α < rho_i,t < β, otherwise 0**



**Where:** 

- **rho_i,t (the probability ratio)** – the **rollout‑to‑train ratio** **rho_i,t = (rollout policy probability) / (training policy probability)**. 
- **α, β (the clipping or masking thresholds)** – hyper‑parameters defining a safe interval. 

The mask keeps the importance weight only when **rho_i,t** lies within **the interval (α, β)**; otherwise the token’s contribution is zeroed, preventing extreme variance from outlier ratios.

The **advantage estimator** uses a leave‑one‑out (LOO) baseline across the **G** sampled responses:

 **advantage A_i,t = Reward R_i minus baseline b_i, where b_i is the average reward of all other rollouts** 

**Where:** 

- **total reward for rollout i** – the **sequence‑level reward** for response **rollout sequence i**. 
- **baseline for rollout i** – the **baseline**, the average reward of all other responses in the batch. 

Because the same advantage is applied to every token in a response, the estimator captures the overall quality of the sampled sequence while reducing variance.

The **stop‑gradient** operator **stop-gradient operator** ensures that gradients do not flow through **rho_i,t**, **r_i,t**, or the mask, so only the policy parameters **θ (model parameters)** receive updates.

**Example:** With **α = 0.5**, **β = 2.0**, and a token where **rho_i,t = 3.5**, the mask outputs **0**, so the token is omitted from the loss. This prevents a single outlier probability ratio from destabilizing training.

### Decoding Configurations: Thinking vs Non‑Thinking 

![](https://storage.googleapis.com/pg_image_bucket/papers/20260329-133331-2603-25040-s-0e289e/table1.png) 
*Table 1: Intern‑S1‑Pro evaluation configurations.*

The two decoding setups differ in four key parameters:

- **max tokens** – longest allowed generation length; a larger limit (65 536) enables extended reasoning chains. 
- **temperature** – controls randomness; 0.8 yields moderate diversity, while 1.0 makes sampling fully stochastic. 
- **top_p** – nucleus‑sampling threshold; 0.95 keeps the cumulative probability mass, encouraging coherent yet varied text. A value of 1.0 disables this filter. 
- **top_k** – limits sampling to the top‑k tokens; 50 restricts the vocabulary to the most likely tokens, whereas 0 removes the filter (equivalent to greedy decoding). 

**Thinking configuration** (long context, moderate randomness, nucleus sampling, limited top‑k) is suited for tasks that require chain‑of‑thought or multimodal reasoning. **Non‑thinking configuration** (shorter context, full randomness, greedy sampling) excels on straightforward fact‑lookup or perception‑driven queries. 

*Example.* Asking “What is the capital of France?” with the non‑thinking settings instantly returns “Paris”. Solving a multi‑step physics problem under the thinking settings produces a detailed, step‑by‑step explanation that can span thousands of tokens. 

The choice of configuration is therefore **benchmark‑specific**, driven by the cognitive demand of each task.

### Conclusion

Intern‑S1‑Pro demonstrates that **trillion‑parameter generalist models** can be turned into powerful scientific assistants when architectural scaling and data curation are tightly coupled. By expanding the expert pool with **Grouped Routing** and stabilizing training through the **STE‑enabled FoPE pipeline**, the model avoids the usual **MoE** pitfalls of expert collapse while efficiently balancing compute across devices.

The specialized caption pipeline added **6 T high‑quality multimodal tokens**, giving the model a nuanced grasp of scientific figures that generic datasets lack. This synergy—scalable architecture plus domain‑focused data—propelled Intern‑S1‑Pro to **state‑of‑the‑art performance** across diverse scientific benchmarks, showcasing robust reasoning and deep domain knowledge.

These results suggest that **scaling generalist foundations** while tailoring data pipelines is a viable path toward accelerating discovery across many scientific fields, opening the door to ever more specialized extensions without sacrificing the benefits of a unified model.
